5 Tips for Writing Better Design Prompts

January 26, 2025 • 7 min read

The difference between getting a professional marketing design and getting abstract art from AI tools often comes down to how you write your prompt. After analyzing thousands of successful LayoutCraft prompts, we've identified the patterns that consistently produce marketing-ready results.

Whether you're creating social media campaigns, email headers, or website banners, these five prompt-writing techniques will help you get exactly what you need on the first try.

Why Prompt Quality Matters for Marketing Designs

Unlike art generators that thrive on ambiguity and interpretation, marketing design AI needs crystal-clear direction. Your prompt is a brief to a designer - the more specific and marketing-focused it is, the better your results.

Poor prompts waste time and produce unusable designs. Great prompts create professional marketing materials ready for immediate use across your campaigns.

1. Lead with Campaign Context and Platform

Always begin your prompt by establishing the marketing context and target platform. This helps LayoutCraft understand both the functional requirements and stylistic expectations for your design.

Platform-Specific Considerations

❌ Generic and Vague:

"Make something about a sale with technology"

✅ Platform and Context Specific:

"LinkedIn banner for B2B software company announcing new product launch. Professional audience of startup founders and CTOs."

By specifying LinkedIn, LayoutCraft knows to create a horizontal layout with professional styling. Mentioning B2B software and the target audience (founders, CTOs) guides color choices, typography, and overall tone toward business-appropriate design.

Campaign Type Context

Different marketing campaigns need different approaches:

  • Product Launches: Focus on features, benefits, excitement
  • Sales Promotions: Emphasize urgency, value, clear offers
  • Brand Awareness: Highlight values, mission, company personality
  • Event Promotion: Include dates, locations, key speakers/attractions
  • Content Marketing: Educational tone, thought leadership positioning

Example with Campaign Context:

"Instagram story for Black Friday sale campaign. E-commerce fashion brand targeting women 25-35. Need urgency and excitement while maintaining brand elegance."

2. Specify Exact Text and Hierarchy

One of LayoutCraft's biggest advantages over art generators is readable, properly formatted text. But you need to tell it exactly what text to include and how to prioritize different text elements.

Text Hierarchy Framework

Think like a marketer when structuring your text hierarchy:

  • Primary Headline: Main value proposition or offer
  • Secondary Text: Supporting details or benefits
  • Call-to-Action: What you want viewers to do next
  • Fine Print: Legal requirements, dates, conditions

❌ Unclear Text Direction:

"Summer sale design with discount information and call-to-action"

✅ Specific Text and Hierarchy:

"Email header for summer sale campaign. Primary headline: 'SUMMER BLOWOUT SALE' in large, bold text. Secondary text: 'Up to 70% Off Everything' in medium size. Call-to-action button: 'Shop Now' in contrasting color. Small text at bottom: 'Sale ends July 31st - Free shipping over $50'"

Marketing Copy That Works

Include proven marketing copy patterns in your prompts:

  • Value Propositions: "Save 40% on premium software"
  • Urgency: "Limited time offer - Ends Friday"
  • Social Proof: "Join 10,000+ satisfied customers"
  • Benefits: "Boost productivity by 50%"
  • Risk Reduction: "30-day money-back guarantee"

Example Marketing-Focused Prompt:

"Facebook ad for project management software. Main headline: 'Stop Juggling Projects' in bold. Subheadline: 'Organize everything in one place'. Benefit bullet points: '• Team collaboration • Task automation • Real-time reporting'. CTA button: 'Start Free Trial'. Trust element: '30-day guarantee' in small text."

3. Define Brand Personality and Visual Style

Marketing designs must align with your brand personality and target audience expectations. Use descriptive terms that convey both visual style and brand character.

Brand Personality Keywords

Professional/Corporate:

  • Clean, minimalist, sophisticated, trustworthy
  • Corporate blue, navy, gray color palette
  • Sans-serif typography, plenty of white space

Startup/Tech:

  • Modern, innovative, cutting-edge, dynamic
  • Bright blues, greens, gradients
  • Bold typography, geometric elements

Creative/Agency:

  • Bold, creative, artistic, unique
  • Vibrant colors, unexpected combinations
  • Mixed typography, creative layouts

Health/Wellness:

  • Calming, natural, trustworthy, caring
  • Soft greens, blues, earth tones
  • Gentle typography, organic shapes

Example with Brand Personality:

"Twitter header for fintech startup. Modern and trustworthy personality that appeals to young professionals. Use clean lines, sophisticated color palette of navy blue and gold. Typography should feel approachable but professional - like a premium banking app."

4. Specify Colors Strategically for Marketing Impact

Color choices in marketing design aren't just aesthetic - they impact conversion rates, brand recognition, and audience response. Be strategic about color requirements.

Marketing Psychology of Colors

Red: Urgency, excitement, appetite (great for sales, food, entertainment)

Blue: Trust, professionalism, stability (ideal for B2B, finance, healthcare)

Green: Growth, health, money (perfect for wellness, finance, sustainability)

Orange: Friendliness, creativity, enthusiasm (good for lifestyle, education)

Purple: Luxury, creativity, sophistication (works for beauty, premium products)

Color Specification Methods

Brand Colors:

"Use our brand colors: primary blue (#1E40AF), secondary gray (#6B7280), accent orange (#F59E0B) for call-to-action elements"

Psychology-Based Colors:

"Use trust-building colors like navy blue and white for this financial services ad. Avoid bright or playful colors that might undermine credibility"

Competitor Differentiation:

"Avoid typical tech company blues and grays. Use warm, approachable colors like soft greens and creams to stand out from competitors"

5. Add Marketing Constraints and Success Criteria

The best marketing design prompts include constraints and success criteria that ensure the final design serves business objectives, not just aesthetic preferences.

Audience-Specific Constraints

Different audiences need different design approaches:

B2B Executive Audience:

"Design must look professional enough for C-suite presentation. Avoid playful elements, bright colors, or casual fonts. Include data/statistics to build credibility."

Consumer/B2C Audience:

"Should feel approachable and friendly for busy parents. Avoid corporate stiffness. Include emotional benefits, not just features. Easy to understand at a glance."

Platform-Specific Constraints

Mobile-First Design:

"Design will primarily be viewed on mobile devices. Text must be readable at small sizes. Avoid complex layouts or tiny details that disappear on phone screens."

LinkedIn Professional Network:

"For LinkedIn audience of marketing professionals. Tone should be authoritative but not stuffy. Include industry credibility markers. Avoid overly sales-y language."

Campaign Performance Criteria

Include success metrics that matter for your campaign:

Click-Through Rate Focus:

"CTA button must stand out prominently - this design is for driving website traffic. Use contrasting colors and clear action words. Avoid cluttered layouts that compete with the main call-to-action."

Brand Awareness Focus:

"Logo and company name must be clearly visible and memorable. Design should be distinctive enough to build brand recognition. Include brand colors consistently."

Conversion Rate Focus:

"Include trust signals like testimonials, security badges, or money-back guarantees. Reduce friction by keeping messaging simple and benefits clear."

Complete Marketing-Focused Prompt Example

"LinkedIn sponsored post for B2B marketing automation software. Target audience: Marketing directors at companies with 50-500 employees. Brand personality: Professional but approachable, innovative but trustworthy. Primary headline: 'Stop Manual Marketing Tasks' in bold, attention-grabbing font. Secondary text: 'Automate email sequences, lead scoring, and campaign tracking in one platform' Call-to-action: 'Book Free Demo' in contrasting button color. Trust element: 'Used by 500+ Marketing Teams' in smaller text. Visual style: Clean, modern design with plenty of white space. Use professional blue (#1E40AF) as primary color, light gray (#F9FAFB) as background, orange (#F59E0B) for CTA button. Include subtle marketing icons or charts to reinforce the automation message. Constraints: Must be readable on mobile LinkedIn app. Avoid overwhelming busy marketing professionals with too much information. Focus on one clear benefit and one clear action."

Advanced Prompt Techniques for Better Results

Iteration and Refinement Strategy

Don't expect perfection on the first try. Use LayoutCraft's regenerate feature strategically to improve your results:

Start Broad, Then Narrow:

  1. First prompt: "LinkedIn banner for software company product launch"
  2. See result, then refine: "LinkedIn banner for project management software launch. Professional blue and white colors. Text: 'Introducing TaskFlow - Project Management Simplified'"
  3. Perfect it: "LinkedIn banner for project management software launch targeting startup founders. Professional but modern style with navy blue (#1E40AF) and white. Main headline: 'Introducing TaskFlow' in large, bold font. Subheadline: 'Project Management Simplified' in smaller text. Include subtle workflow icons. Clean, minimalist design that builds trust with business audience."

A/B Testing Prompt Variations

Create multiple versions for testing by changing one element at a time:

Version A (Benefit-Focused):

"Facebook ad for productivity app. Headline: 'Get 2 Hours Back Every Day' - focus on time savings benefit."

Version B (Feature-Focused):

"Facebook ad for productivity app. Headline: 'AI-Powered Task Management' - focus on innovative features."

Common Prompt Mistakes That Kill Marketing Designs

1. Art-Focused Instead of Marketing-Focused Language

❌ Art Generator Thinking:

"Create a beautiful, artistic representation of our software with flowing lines and creative interpretation"

✅ Marketing Generator Thinking:

"Create a professional banner that clearly communicates our software's main benefit to busy business owners"

2. Forgetting the Target Audience

❌ Audience-Agnostic:

"Make a poster for our fitness app"

✅ Audience-Specific:

"Instagram post for fitness app targeting busy professionals who want quick workouts. Emphasize time efficiency and results."

3. Vague Call-to-Action Instructions

❌ Unclear Action:

"Include some kind of call-to-action"

✅ Specific Action:

"Prominent orange button with 'Start Free Trial' in white text. Button should be the most eye-catching element after the headline."

4. Ignoring Platform Context

❌ Platform-Agnostic:

"Social media post for our product launch"

✅ Platform-Optimized:

"Instagram carousel post for product launch. Each slide should work independently but tell a cohesive story. Optimized for mobile viewing with large, readable text."

Measuring Prompt Success

Great prompts consistently produce designs that achieve marketing objectives. Track these indicators:

Design Quality Indicators

  • First-try success: Usable design without major edits needed
  • Text readability: All text elements clearly legible
  • Brand alignment: Matches your brand guidelines and personality
  • Platform appropriateness: Looks native to the target platform

Marketing Performance Indicators

  • Clear value proposition: Main benefit immediately obvious
  • Strong call-to-action: Next step clearly defined and prominent
  • Audience appropriateness: Resonates with target demographic
  • Conversion elements: Includes trust signals, urgency, or social proof as needed

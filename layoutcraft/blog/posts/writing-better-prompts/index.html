5 Tips for Writing Better Design Prompts

January 26, 2025 • 4 min read

The difference between getting exactly what you want from LayoutCraft and getting something "close enough" often comes down to how you write your prompt. Here are five tips to help you write prompts that get results.

1. Start with the Format and Purpose

Always begin your prompt by stating what you're creating and why. This helps LayoutCraft understand the context and constraints of your design.

❌ Vague:

"Make something with AI and technology"

✅ Specific:

"A blog header for an article about AI in healthcare technology"

By specifying "blog header," LayoutCraft knows to create a horizontal layout with space for text overlay. By mentioning the topic, it can choose appropriate imagery and color schemes.

2. Be Explicit About Text Content

One of LayoutCraft's strengths is readable, well-placed text. But you need to tell it exactly what text to include.

❌ Vague:

"Summer sale design with discount information"

✅ Specific:

"Instagram post for summer sale. Main text: 'SUMMER SALE' in large letters. Subtext: '50% Off Everything'. Small text at bottom: 'June 1-15 Only'"

Include the exact wording, specify hierarchy (main text, subtext), and mention where text should appear if you have preferences.

3. Define the Mood and Style

LayoutCraft can adapt to different visual styles, but it needs direction. Use descriptive adjectives to set the tone.

Style words that work well:

  • Professional: corporate, clean, minimalist, modern
  • Energetic: vibrant, dynamic, bold, exciting
  • Elegant: luxurious, sophisticated, premium, refined
  • Friendly: warm, approachable, playful, casual
  • Tech-focused: futuristic, digital, innovative, cutting-edge

Example prompt with mood:

"LinkedIn banner for a financial advisor. Professional and trustworthy mood. Navy blue and gold colors. Text: 'John Smith, CFP - Building Your Financial Future'"

4. Specify Colors When They Matter

If you have brand colors or specific preferences, include them. LayoutCraft understands color names, hex codes, and descriptive terms.

Ways to specify colors:

  • Basic colors: "red", "blue", "green"
  • Descriptive colors: "forest green", "navy blue", "coral pink"
  • Color schemes: "earth tones", "pastel palette", "monochromatic blue"
  • Brand references: "Spotify green", "Facebook blue" (for well-known brands)

Example with color guidance:

"Social media post for eco-friendly product launch. Use natural earth tones - greens, browns, and cream. Avoid bright or neon colors."

5. Add Context and Constraints

The more context you provide, the better LayoutCraft can tailor the design. Mention your audience, platform, or any specific requirements.

Helpful context to include:

  • Target audience: "for young professionals", "for parents", "for tech enthusiasts"
  • Platform specifics: "Instagram story", "YouTube thumbnail", "email header"
  • Brand personality: "playful startup", "established law firm", "innovative tech company"
  • Must-avoid elements: "no people", "no dark colors", "no complicated patterns"

Example with full context:

"Blog header for a parenting website article about healthy lunch ideas. Warm and friendly style with soft colors. Include the text 'Quick & Healthy School Lunches' as the main headline. Target audience is busy parents. Avoid cluttered designs - keep it clean and easy to read."

Bonus Tip: Iterate and Refine

Don't expect perfection on the first try. Use LayoutCraft's regenerate feature to create variations, and refine your prompt based on what works. Each generation helps you understand how to communicate better with the AI.

Start simple: "Blog header about coffee"
See result, then refine: "Blog header about artisan coffee brewing. Minimalist style. Text: 'The Art of Pour-Over Coffee'"
Perfect it: "Blog header about artisan coffee brewing. Minimalist style with warm brown tones. Text: 'The Art of Pour-Over Coffee' in elegant serif font. Include subtle coffee bean imagery."
